Derivative-Based Edge Detection

Introduction

Image segmentation is the division of an image into regions or categories, which correspond to different objects or parts of objects. Every pixel in an image is allocated to one of these categories.

A good segmentation is typically one in which:

  • Pixels in the same category have similar grey scale or multivariate values and form a connected region.
  • Neighboring pixels in different categories have dissimilar values.

Edge Based Image Segmentation

Edge-based segmentation is one of the most popular implementations of segmentation in image processing. It focuses on identifying the edges of different objects in an image. This is a crucial step because edges contain important information about object boundaries and features.

Edge detection helps remove unwanted and unnecessary information from the image. It reduces the image size considerably, making it easier to analyse. Edge-based algorithms identify edges according to differences in texture, contrast, grey level, colour, saturation, and other properties.

Gradient

In image processing, a gradient refers to the rate of change of intensity in an image. It points in the direction of the greatest rate of increase of intensity, and its magnitude represents the strength of change.

∇f(x, y) = [ ∂f/∂x , ∂f/∂y ]

Where:

  • ∂f/∂x: Partial derivative in the horizontal direction.
  • ∂f/∂y: Partial derivative in the vertical direction.

The magnitude of the gradient is given by:

|∇f(x, y)| = √((∂f/∂x)² + (∂f/∂y)²)

The direction of the gradient is:

θ(x, y) = tan⁻¹((∂f/∂y) / (∂f/∂x))

Steps in Edge Detection

  1. Filtering: Filtering is used to reduce noise before edge detection. More filtering reduces noise, but it can also reduce edge strength.
  2. Enhancement: Enhancement highlights pixels where there is a significant change in local intensity. This is usually done by computing gradient magnitude.
  3. Detection: Detection selects only strong edge points. Thresholding is commonly used to decide which points are actual edges.
  4. Localization: Localization estimates the exact position and orientation of the edge.

First Order Derivative Operators

First-order derivative operators are used to detect edges by measuring the rate of change in intensity. These operators highlight regions with strong intensity contrast, which usually represent object boundaries.

1. Roberts Operator

The Roberts cross operator provides a simple approximation to the gradient magnitude.

G[f[i,j]] = |f[i,j] - f[i+1,j+1]| + |f[i+1,j] - f[i,j+1]|

Second Order Derivative Operators

Second-order derivative operators detect edges by finding zero crossings in the second derivative of image intensity. These operators are useful for identifying points where intensity changes sharply.

Laplacian Operator

The Laplacian is the two-dimensional equivalent of the second derivative.

∇²f(x, y) = ∂²f/∂x² + ∂²f/∂y²

Comparison of Edge Detectors

Edge Detector Method Advantages Limitations
Roberts Gradient Based
  1. Simple and fast computation.
  2. Useful for sharp edges.
  1. Highly sensitive to noise.
  2. Less accurate for complex images.
Sobel Gradient Based
  1. Better noise resistance than Roberts.
  2. Detects horizontal and vertical edges effectively.
  1. May produce thick edges.
  2. Less accurate than Canny.
Prewitt Gradient Based
  1. Simple implementation.
  2. Good for basic edge detection.
  1. Less noise-resistant than Sobel.
  2. Not suitable for highly noisy images.
Canny Gaussian Based
  1. Accurate edge detection.
  2. Good noise reduction.
  3. Produces thin and clear edges.
  1. Slow compared to simple operators.
  2. More complex implementation.
